I finished my service on a Thursday. By Monday I was back in the lab, staring at a codebase I hadn't touched in eighteen months, trying to remember what I knew.

The transition was stranger than I expected. Not the re-learning — that was just time. The stranger part was realizing how much of what I'd learned in the army had no obvious translation into the vocabulary of a research lab. Leadership. Patience under ambiguity. How to give instructions that actually stick. These felt important, but I didn't have the words for why.

## What KATUSA actually is

KATUSA stands for Korean Augmentation to the United States Army. The program embeds Korean soldiers into U.S. Army units — we live and work alongside American soldiers, operating in a bilingual environment where most official communication is in English and most unofficial communication is in Korean.

You are, in practice, a translator. Not just of language — of context, of expectation, of what each side finds obvious and what each side finds inexplicable. After a few months, you stop thinking of this as a special skill. It just becomes how you think.

> "The hardest translation isn't between languages. It's between assumptions."

## On leading a small team

In my last six months, I led a small group of KATUSA soldiers. I had not planned for this. I was not particularly good at it, at first.

The main thing I learned: the gap between knowing something and being able to explain it is where most leadership problems live. I could do the task. Explaining *why* the task mattered in this specific way, for this specific mission, in terms that made sense to someone two years younger with a different set of experiences — that was the actual work.

## What carried over

I came back to research expecting to feel behind. I did, technically. But something had shifted in how I approached problems.

In the army, ambiguity is the normal condition. Orders are incomplete. Information arrives late or contradicts itself. You make a decision with what you have, and then you update. I'd always treated uncertainty in research as a problem to be solved before proceeding. Now I treat it as the medium I work in.

I don't know if that's wisdom or just a different kind of recklessness. But the policy converges faster when you're not waiting for the loss curve to be perfect before you commit to a direction.
